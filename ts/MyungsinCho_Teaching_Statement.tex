\begin{center}\LARGE{Teaching Statement}\end{center}

My teaching experience spans a wide range of settings, from lower-division service courses to upper-division courses, and from virtual instruction to large lecture classes. Across these settings, I have taught students with very different levels of preparation, interests, and goals. Working with such a broad range of students has taught me to adjust the pace, level, and emphasis of a course to the audience in front of me, while keeping the larger purpose of mathematics teaching in view.

Teaching across this range has also made me think carefully about what students should take away from a mathematics course. In introductory and general-education settings especially, students are often forming their basic understanding of what mathematics is and how it is learned. That has made me take these courses seriously not only as places to teach technical material, but also as places to show students that mathematics has structure, meaning, and a way of thinking behind it. Even at the introductory level, students benefit when they are treated as capable of understanding ideas, not just procedures.

\paragraph{What I Want Students to Learn}\quad

My central goal as an instructor is to help students learn how to think mathematically. Of course students need to acquire techniques, but I do not want them to experience mathematics as a list of rules to memorize and reproduce. I want them to see that definitions have meaning, that methods come from structure, and that solving a problem is often a matter of understanding how the pieces fit together. In class, I try to emphasize not only what is true, but why a particular way of thinking is useful.

For this reason, I usually begin with examples and concrete situations before moving to formal language. Once students understand what a definition is trying to capture, the associated methods become easier to remember and apply. In calculus, for instance, I often frame the course through the idea of \emph{approximation}. Topics that can initially seem separate then become parts of a single picture. More generally, I try to show students the strength of abstraction. Many students are wary of abstract mathematics, especially in courses that are not explicitly proof-based. I think it is important to show them that abstraction is not a matter of unnecessary generality, but a way of organizing ideas clearly and using them efficiently.

I also want students to become comfortable explaining mathematical ideas in a precise way. In introductory courses, many students have never been taught how to write a mathematical argument, even at a basic level. To help with this, I prepare worksheets in which parts of an explanation are already in place and students fill in the missing steps. The goal is not to demand polished proofs from the outset, but to help students become familiar with the grammar of mathematical reasoning: how definitions are used, how a claim is broken into steps, and how an explanation is organized. I have found that this kind of structured practice makes students more willing to engage with mathematical writing, and students have often told me that these worksheets were one of the most helpful parts of the course.

\paragraph{Course Design and Classroom Practice}\quad

In practice, I try to keep a close connection between lecture, homework, and assessment. I also write homework problems myself when appropriate, so that they reflect the material as it is presented in class. This helps students experience the course as a coherent whole rather than as a sequence of separate tasks.

I also ask students questions throughout class so that they are not simply watching the lecture unfold. This is especially important in large courses, where it is easy to become passive. Some questions check basic understanding, while others ask students to compare methods, anticipate the next step, or explain the reason behind a calculation. These small moments of participation make the class more active and help me see more clearly where students need further support.

\paragraph{Assessment and Standards}\quad

I try to make my expectations clear and to design assessments that reflect them closely. In particular, I prefer to assess students through several exams or quizzes over the course of the semester rather than placing too much weight on a single test. When possible, I also weight stronger exam performances more heavily, so that one poor result does not define a student’s standing in the course. This helps students stay engaged after a difficult exam and gives them a real incentive to keep studying for the next one.

I also think carefully about the balance between theory and computation. I want students to engage with general ideas, but I do not want the course to be passable only for students who are already comfortable with abstract reasoning or proof. For that reason, I design homework and exam problems so that students who have understood the main ideas and can carry out the basic computations can succeed, while stronger students still have room to go further.

When possible, I also allow students to correct mistakes on exams for partial credit. This encourages them to return to problems they missed and study them again, rather than moving on once the exam is over. In this way, assessment becomes part of the learning process rather than simply a final judgment.


\paragraph{Mentoring and Individual Support}\quad

Beyond the classroom, I place a high value on mentoring and individual guidance. Through the \emph{Directed Reading Program} at Indiana, I worked with undergraduates on topics such as algebraic topology and category theory. I set weekly goals, asked students to write short summaries, and encouraged them to present what they had learned in a way that others could follow. This helped students build independence and, in several cases, gave them the confidence to move on to graduate-level coursework.

I also see office hours and one-on-one conversations as an important part of teaching. Students often need more than a second explanation of the material. Sometimes they need help identifying exactly where their understanding breaks down, and sometimes they simply need reassurance that struggling with a concept is part of learning mathematics. I try to make myself approachable and to create an atmosphere in which students feel comfortable asking basic questions as well as more advanced ones. In my experience, this kind of steady support can change how a student experiences the entire course.

\paragraph{Conclusion}\quad

Across different courses and teaching environments, my aim has remained the same. I want students to learn mathematics in a way that is clear, serious, and understandable. I want them to acquire technical skill, but also to see the structure behind what they are doing and to gain confidence in their ability to follow mathematical reasoning for themselves.

For many students, these may be the last mathematics courses they take, and I want them to leave with more than a set of techniques. I want them to leave with some sense of the logic and coherence of the subject, and with the feeling that mathematics is something they can work through carefully and understand. Whether I am teaching a large lecture, a service course, or a small reading course, I see teaching as guiding students toward clearer reasoning and a more durable understanding of mathematics. \\
